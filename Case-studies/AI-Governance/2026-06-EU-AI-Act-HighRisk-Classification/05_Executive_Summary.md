# Executive Summary
## EU AI Act — Draft Guidance on Which AI Systems Count as "High-Risk"

**Audience:** CISO / Board | **Date:** 2026-06-08 | **Risk Rating:** Medium (Compliance Readiness)

---

**What happened:**
European regulators released draft guidance clarifying exactly which AI systems will be considered "high-risk" under the EU's AI law — and therefore subject to the strictest requirements (formal assessments, documentation, registration, and ongoing oversight). They are also taking public feedback on this guidance through June 23, and confirmed they're standing up dedicated teams to enforce the law going forward. Separately, the toughest deadlines have been pushed back to late 2027 and 2028 — giving organizations more runway, but the direction of travel is clear and firming up.

**Why it matters:**
Any AI tool we use or build that EU-based customers, employees, or partners interact with — internal AI assistants, hiring tools, customer-service bots, or anything that makes decisions affecting people — could fall into this "high-risk" bucket. Getting the classification wrong in either direction costs us: too cautious, and we waste resources on unnecessary paperwork; too lax, and we face a compressed scramble — and potentially significant fines — once enforcement ramps up.

**What we're doing:**
We are using this window to take stock: cataloguing every AI system that could touch EU users, and testing each one against the new draft criteria now, while there's still time to plan rather than react. Where the guidance is genuinely unclear, we're considering submitting feedback directly to the regulators — a rare chance to help shape the rules before they're locked in.

**Bottom line for leadership:**
Nothing is on fire — the deadlines that matter are roughly 18 months out. But the organizations that get caught flat-footed will be the ones that didn't start cataloguing and classifying their AI systems until the deadline was imminent. Doing this work now, calmly and on our own timeline, costs a fraction of doing it later under regulatory pressure — and it doubles as evidence for our broader AI-governance certification goals (ISO 42001).
